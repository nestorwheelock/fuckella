## Analysis of Key Differences

### 1. Rent: $10,000 → $13,500 (35% increase)
Obviously not "the same." But more importantly, this is the BAIT-AND-SWITCH amount. The landlord communicated $12,500 on 2/24/26, then presented $13,500 at the signing on 3/3/26. So the contract is different from what was signed before AND different from what was communicated.

### 2. Late Interest: 6% → 10% per month
Both rates are usurious under Art. 2659 (legal interest is 12%/year = 1%/month). But the new contract nearly DOUBLES the already-illegal rate. She changed a predatory clause to make it MORE predatory.

### 3. Clause 6: "Good Conditions" → "Perfect Working Condition"
This is subtle but significant. The old contract said the tenant acknowledges receiving the property in "good conditions of use." The new one says "perfect working condition." Given that the water heater, AC units, shower drain, and other items are BROKEN, signing a statement that everything is in "perfect working condition" would be signing a lie — and waiving all repair claims.

### 4. Clause 8: "Inoperantes" → "Imperantes" (Typo that reverses meaning)
The old contract correctly says unauthorized subletting would be "null and **ineffective** against the Landlord." The new one says "null and **prevailing** against the Landlord" — which is the OPPOSITE meaning and makes no legal sense. This appears to be a careless copy error. It's evidence of sloppy drafting at best.

### 5. Clause 13: Fixed a Drafting Error in Landlord's Favor
The old contract's penalty clause has an ambiguous subject: "pagará la Pena Convencional" — grammatically, the subject could be read as the Landlord (the preceding sentence discusses the Landlord promoting eviction). The new contract explicitly clarifies: "los Arrendatarios pagarán" (the Tenants shall pay). She noticed a drafting error that potentially benefited the tenants and FIXED it.
