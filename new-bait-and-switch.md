## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

A **foreign tenant** who does not speak Spanish signed a notarized lease agreement (deed before a Notary Public) in Cancun, Quintana Roo. The contract:

- Was drafted **entirely in Spanish** with no translation provided
- Was **rushed** at the notary's office with no interpreter present
- Was signed before the tenant could understand its terms
- The **tenant paid the notary's fees**, yet the **landlord chose the notary** (conflict of interest)
- The tenant **never received a copy** of the signed contract

The landlord now claims the contract has expired and demands a rent increase, threatening eviction if the tenant does not accept. Meanwhile:

- The landlord made **written promises via WhatsApp** (air conditioning replacement, furniture removal) and broke them
- The tenant has made **extensive repairs** that were the landlord's legal responsibility
- The landlord continues **accepting rent at the original amount**
- The air conditioning system replacement took **3 months** — in the tropical heat of Cancun

**The law overwhelmingly favors the tenant.** The Quintana Roo Civil Code provides:

1. **Art. 383(d):** The expression of will **does not exist** when a person does not know the language of the contract — potentially invalidating the entire contract (absolute nullity)
2. **Art. 193-205:** Consent obtained through error, fraud, or deliberate concealment (reticence) is invalid
3. **Art. 2739:** The lease has automatically renewed for an **indefinite term** (tacit renewal) at the **original rent amount**
4. **Art. 2687:** The landlord's maintenance obligations are **mandatory by law** and cannot be waived by contract
5. **Art. 2698:** Tenant protection provisions are **non-waivable** ("any stipulation to the contrary shall be null")
6. **Art. 87, 98, 220:** The notary bears **professional liability** for failing to verify comprehension

The tenant has multiple defenses against eviction and strong counterclaims for reimbursement, damages, and potential nullity of abusive contract clauses.

## 3. APPLICABLE LEGISLATION — VERBATIM ARTICLE TEXT

All citations below are **verbatim** from the Civil Code for the State of Quintana Roo (2023 edition).

### 3.1 Consent and Will (Defects of Will)

**Article 193.** Consent is not valid if given by error, by fear, or by fraud.

**Article 194.** Error regarding the nature of the legal transaction annuls it, if one of the parties believes they have entered into a particular contract while the other party believes the one entered into is a different transaction.

**Article 198.** Fraud in legal transactions is understood as any machination or artifice used to induce error or maintain in error any of those who enter into the transaction, and, in bad faith, the concealment of the error of one of them once known.

**Article 203.** Ignorance or lack of knowledge regarding the subject matter of the transaction is a cause of nullity when it induces error in the person who suffers it.

**Article 204.** Reticence is also a cause of nullity if it induces error.

**Article 205.** Reticence is the silence voluntarily maintained by one of the parties regarding a fact or circumstance that the other would have an interest in knowing in order to be in a conscious position to enter into the transaction.

**Article 211.** It is not lawful to waive in advance the nullity resulting from fraud, bad faith, reticence, or violence.

### 3.2 Expression of Will — Language Requirement

**Article 383.** The expression of will does not exist if the person expressing it is:

a) A child under seven years of age;

b) A person deprived of intelligence by insanity, idiocy, or imbecility, even if they have lucid intervals, except in the latter case, by provision of law;

c) A deaf-mute who cannot read or write, nor make themselves understood through an interpreter using the written or sign language taught in schools for their class;

**d) Persons who cannot read or write, nor know the language in which they may appear to have contracted.**

### 3.3 Classification of Nullity

**Article 384.** The absence of any of the requirements mentioned in Article 135 produces absolute nullity of the act, which would operate by operation of law if there is no commencement or beginning of performance.

**Article 385.** If there is a commencement of performance, absolute nullity must necessarily be declared in court.

**Article 386.** Illegality in the object, motive, purpose, or condition of the legal transaction produces its absolute nullity; but such nullity is relative when, by provision of law, it does not meet all the characteristics enumerated in Article 388.

**Article 387.** Relative nullity of the legal transaction also arises: I.- From legal incapacity of the author or one of the parties, provided it does not involve the persons enumerated in Article 383. II.- From defects of will; III.- Because the will was not expressed in the form required by law; and IV.- From lesion.

**Article 388.** Absolute nullity may be invoked by anyone with a legitimate interest in asserting it; it is imprescriptible; it is not extinguished by express or tacit waiver nor by confirmation or ratification, and courts may consider it even on their own motion.

**Article 390.** Absolute nullity occurs by operation of law and by the mere fact that the violation is committed.

**Article 394.** Nullity based on defects of will, lesion, or incapacity that is not absolute may only be invoked by the person who suffered those defects, was harmed by the lesion, or is the incapacitated person.

**Article 398.** The action of nullity based on error prescribes within two years, unless the person who incurred it discovers it before that period expires, in which case the action prescribes within ninety days from the day the error was discovered.

**Article 401.** A legal transaction vitiated by nullity only in part is not totally null if its component parts can legally subsist separately, unless it is demonstrated that at the time of entering into the transaction, the intent was that it should subsist only in its entirety.

### 3.4 Notary Duties

**Article 220.** Whenever the law requires or the parties voluntarily wish that a private document be ratified before a Notary, Registrar, Judge of First Instance, Minor Court, Justice of the Peace, or administrative authority, these officials must, in their respective cases, verify the identity of the parties, their capacity, and the authenticity of the signatures.

### 3.5 Civil Liability

**Article 87.** Any act of a person that does not constitute a crime, executed through fault or negligence, whether simple or willful, that causes damage to another in their person or property, obliges the author to repair the damage and compensate for the losses in accordance with the provisions of this Code.

**Article 98.** Persons practicing technical or liberal professions are obligated to answer for the damages and losses caused in the performance of their duties by their assistants, helpers, collaborators, interns, patients, or employees.

**Article 104.** The owner of a building is liable for damages resulting from the ruin of all or part of it, if such ruin occurs due to lack of necessary repairs or construction defects.

### 3.6 Public Order and Protection of the Weaker Party

**Article 8.** The will of private parties cannot exempt from compliance with the law, nor alter or modify it. Only private rights that do not directly affect public interest may be waived, when the waiver does not harm third-party rights.

**Article 13.** Legal acts executed contrary to prohibitive laws or public order shall be null, except in cases where the law provides otherwise.

**Article 21.** In case of conflict of rights, in the absence of an express applicable law, the controversy shall be decided in favor of the party seeking to avoid harm and not in favor of the party seeking to obtain profit. If the conflict is between equal rights or rights of the same kind, it shall be decided observing the greatest possible equality among the parties, and in favor of the weaker party.

**Article 23.** Protection of public order shall include the protection of the economically, socially, or culturally weak.

**Article 24.** A legal act shall be null in which someone, exploiting the extreme ignorance, notorious inexperience, or extreme poverty of another, obtains excessive profit that is evidently disproportionate.

### 3.7 Lease — Landlord's Obligations

**Article 2682.** The lease must be executed in writing and its validity begins, unless otherwise agreed, with the delivery of the property.

**Article 2687.** The landlord is obligated, even without express agreement:

I.- To maintain the leased property in a condition fit for its agreed use, and absent agreement on this point, for the use to which the property by its very nature is intended, making all necessary repairs for this purpose;

II.- Not to hinder or obstruct in any way the use of the leased property or the provision of basic services, except for urgent and indispensable repairs.

III.- To guarantee the peaceful use and enjoyment of the property for the entire term of the contract; and

IV.- To answer for the damages and losses suffered by the tenant due to defects or hidden defects of the property, existing prior to the lease.

**Article 2688.** The landlord may not, during the lease, alter the form of the leased property or interfere with its legitimate use, except in the case designated in section II of the preceding article, for which purpose the tenant must notify the landlord as soon as possible of the need for repairs, under penalty of paying for the damages and losses caused by the omission.

If the landlord fails to make the necessary repairs for the intended use of the property, the tenant may choose to demand rescission of the lease or to petition the court to compel the landlord to fulfill their obligation, through the expedited procedure established in the Code of Civil Procedure; with the understanding that if the landlord does not make the repairs within the period set by the judge in execution of judgment, the judge may authorize the tenant to make them at the expense of rent.

The judge, according to the circumstances of the case, shall decide on the payment of damages caused to the tenant by the lack of timely repairs.

**Article 2692.** The landlord is liable for defects of the leased property that prevent its use, even if the landlord was unaware of them or they arose without fault of the tenant during the lease. In this case, the tenant may request a reduction of rent or rescission of the contract, unless it is proven that the tenant was aware of the defects before or at the time of entering into the lease.

**Article 2694.** The landlord is required to pay for improvements made by the tenant:

I.- If in the contract, or subsequently, the landlord authorized them and undertook to pay for them;

II.- If they are useful improvements and the contract is rescinded due to the landlord's fault;

III.- When the contract is for an indefinite term, if the landlord authorized the tenant to make improvements and terminates the lease before enough time has passed for the tenant to be compensated through use of the improvements for the expenses incurred.

The improvements referred to in sections II and III must be paid by the landlord, notwithstanding any stipulation in the contract that the improvements shall remain for the benefit of the leased property.

### 3.8 Tenant's Obligations (Limited)

**Article 2695.** The tenant is obligated:

V.- To make repairs for minor deterioration of the property or its accessories that are regularly caused by inhabiting a house;

### 3.9 Habitability and Non-Waivable Provisions

**Article 2698.** If due to an act of God or force majeure the tenant is totally prevented from using the leased property, no rent shall accrue while the impediment lasts, and if it lasts more than two months, the tenant may request rescission of the contract. However, if for the same reasons use of the property is only partially prevented, the tenant may request a partial reduction of rent, as determined by experts, unless the parties opt for rescission of the contract if the impediment lasts the period set in the preceding paragraph. **The provisions of this article are non-waivable.**

**Article 2707.** A tenant who loses total use of the property due to repairs has the right not to pay rent and to request rescission of the contract, while if the loss of use is not total but partial, the tenant may only request an equitable reduction of rent; but may also demand rescission if such partial loss of use lasts more than two months.

**Article 2709.** A premises that does not meet the hygiene and health conditions required by the Health Code may not be leased. A landlord who fails to make the repairs ordered by the relevant authorities as necessary for a premises to be habitable and hygienic is liable for the damages and losses suffered by the tenants for that reason. The right to collect this compensation cannot be waived in advance.

### 3.10 Lease Renewal and Termination

**Article 2714.** All leases of rural or urban properties that have not been entered into for an expressly determined period shall terminate at the will of either contracting party, with prior notice to the other party given in an unequivocal form **two months in advance if the property is urban**, and one year in advance if it is rural.

**Article 2738.** No later than fifteen days after a lease contract expires, the tenant shall have the right, provided they are current on rent payments, to request that the contract be extended for up to one year. The landlord may increase the previous rent by up to ten percent, provided they demonstrate that rents in the relevant area have experienced increases that justify the increase.

Exempt from the obligation to extend the lease are owners who wish to inhabit the house, personally occupy the premises intended for an office, industry, or commerce, or cultivate the property whose lease has expired, with the understanding that if three months after the final judgment the landlord has not inhabited the house, cultivated the property, or personally occupied the premises intended for an office, commerce, or industry, they shall be ordered, through the summary procedure specified in the relevant Code, to deliver to the tenant, by way of compensation, the equivalent of the rent for the number of months set at the discretion of the Judge or Court, which may not exceed twelve months.

**Article 2739.** If after the lease and its extension, if any, have ended, the tenant continues without opposition in the enjoyment and use of the property, and the property is rural, the contract shall be understood to be renewed for another year, and for an indefinite term if the property is urban. In either case, the tenant shall pay the rent corresponding to the time exceeding the contract term, in accordance with what was being paid.

**Article 2740.** When there is an extension of the lease or tacit renewal as respectively referred to in the two preceding articles, the obligations granted by a third party for the security of the lease shall cease, unless otherwise agreed.

**Article 2741.** The landlord may demand rescission of the contract:

I.- For failure to pay rent in the terms provided in section I of Article 2683;

II.- For use of the property in violation of the provisions of section III of Article 2695; and

III.- For subletting the property in violation of the provisions of Article 2716.

**Article 2744.** If the leased property is sold judicially, the lease shall subsist, unless it appears that it was entered into within the sixty days prior to the seizure of the property.

### 3.11 Unjust Enrichment and Management of Another's Affairs

**Article 44.** A person who without mandate and without being obligated to do so takes charge of another's affair must act in accordance with the interests of the owner of the affair.

**Article 48.** The owner of an affair who benefits from the management must fulfill the obligations that the manager contracted on their behalf, indemnify the manager for those contracted personally, and reimburse the necessary and useful expenses incurred.

**Article 60.** A person who is enriched without cause at the expense of another is obligated to compensate for the impoverishment to the extent of their enrichment.

**Article 61.** When the enrichment consists of the acquisition of a property, the compensation shall be, at the defendant's election, the price of the property or its restitution.

### 3.12 Statute of Limitations (Prescription Periods)

**Article 2464.** Pensions, annuities, rents, and any other periodic payments not collected at maturity shall prescribe in two years, counted from the maturity of each one.

## 4. LEGAL ANALYSIS

### 4.1 Vitiated Consent — Language Barrier and Foreign Status

**This is the most powerful argument in the entire case.**

#### 4.1.1 The Nuclear Option: Art. 383(d) — The Will Does Not Exist

> **Art. 383(d):** "Persons who cannot read or write, **nor know the language in which they may appear to have contracted.**"

The law states that for such persons, **"the expression of will does not exist"** — the expression of will **does not exist**. This is not merely a defect; it means the will **was never formed**.

**Application to this case:**
- The tenant is a foreign citizen who does not speak Spanish
- The contract was drafted entirely in Spanish
- No translation was provided
- No interpreter was present
- Therefore, the tenant **did not know the language in which they appeared to have contracted**

Under Art. 383(d), the tenant's expression of will **does not exist**. Combined with Art. 384, the absence of an essential requirement (consent/will per Art. 135) produces **absolute nullity**.

**Consequences of Art. 388 regarding absolute nullity:**
- May be invoked by any person with a legitimate interest
- Is **imprescriptible** (never expires)
- Cannot be extinguished by express or tacit waiver
- Cannot be cured by confirmation or ratification
- Courts may consider it **on their own motion** (even without a request)

**Strategic consideration:** Art. 401 provides that a partially null contract may have its valid parts subsist. The tenant may not want to nullify the ENTIRE contract (since the lease itself benefits the tenant). Instead, the tenant can argue that specific abusive clauses (like "tenant is responsible for all repairs") are absolutely null while the beneficial lease terms survive. This is surgical: nullify the bad clauses, keep the lease.

#### 4.1.2 Defects of Will

Even if a court does not apply Art. 383(d) directly, multiple defects of consent exist:

**Error (Art. 193, 203):** The tenant's ignorance of Spanish means they were in fundamental error about the contract terms. Art. 203 specifically states that "ignorance or lack of knowledge regarding the subject matter of the transaction" is a cause of nullity when it induces error.

**Reticence (Art. 204-205):** The landlord's failure to disclose — or ensure the tenant understood — critical contract terms (especially the "all repairs" clause) constitutes **reticence**: "the silence voluntarily maintained by one of the parties regarding a fact or circumstance that the other would have an interest in knowing." The tenant obviously had an interest in knowing that the contract supposedly made them responsible for all repairs.

**Fraud (Art. 198):** Rushing a non-Spanish speaker through a notarized signing conducted entirely in Spanish without translation or interpretation may constitute "machination or artifice used to induce error or maintain in error any of those who enter into the transaction."

**Non-waivable (Art. 211):** "It is not lawful to waive in advance the nullity resulting from fraud, bad faith, reticence, or violence." Any clause in the contract purporting to waive these nullity rights is itself null.

#### 4.1.3 Exploitation of Ignorance (Art. 24)

> "A legal act shall be null in which someone, **exploiting the extreme ignorance, notorious inexperience** or extreme poverty of another, obtains excessive profit that is evidently disproportionate."

A foreign citizen who does not speak Spanish, signing a contract they cannot read, that contains terms making them responsible for all repairs of a property they don't own — this is a textbook case of exploitation of "extreme ignorance" and "notorious inexperience."

#### 4.1.4 Statute of Limitations for Nullity Actions

- **Absolute nullity (Art. 383(d) + 388):** IMPRESCRIPTIBLE. No time limit. Ever.
- **Nullity based on error (Art. 398):** 2 years from the contract, OR 90 days from discovery of the error (whichever is later)
- **Nullity for form (Art. 393):** 2 years from the date the contract was entered into

### 4.2 Breach of Notary Duties

#### 4.2.1 The Notary's Legal Obligation

> **Art. 220:** Officials who ratify documents must "verify the identity of the parties, their capacity, and the authenticity of the signatures."

"Verify" means "to make certain of" or "to confirm." The notary has an affirmative duty to verify not only identity and signature authenticity, but **capacity**. A person who does not understand the language of the document they are signing lacks the practical capacity to consent to its terms.

#### 4.2.2 The Notary's Breach

The notary failed to:
1. **Verify the tenant's understanding** of the Spanish-language document
2. **Require or provide a translator/interpreter** for a visibly foreign, non-Spanish-speaking party
3. **Ensure informed consent** — the very purpose of notarization
4. **Protect the paying client's interests** — the tenant paid the notary's fees

#### 4.2.3 Conflict of Interest

The notary's situation presents a clear conflict:
- The **tenant paid the notary's fees**, establishing the tenant as the notary's client
- The **landlord chose the notary**, creating a trust/loyalty relationship between landlord and notary
- The notary's duty was to **both parties**, but particularly to the paying party
- The notary failed to protect the paying client's interests while facilitating a contract that benefited the party who selected the notary

#### 4.2.4 Professional Liability

> **Art. 87:** "Any act of a person that does not constitute a crime, executed through fault or negligence, whether simple or willful, that causes damage to another in their person or property, obliges the author to repair the damage and compensate for the losses."

> **Art. 98:** "Persons practicing technical or liberal professions are obligated to answer for the damages and losses caused in the performance of their duties."

The notary's negligence in failing to ensure the tenant's understanding caused direct harm: the tenant signed a contract with terms (especially the "all repairs" clause) that they never understood, resulting in thousands of pesos in repair costs that the tenant should never have assumed.

**Available actions:**
- Complaint to the **Quintana Roo Notary College**
- Civil liability lawsuit under Arts. 87 and 98
- Request to the notary for a **certified copy** of the deed (the tenant has an absolute right to this as a party to the instrument)

### 4.3 Failure to Deliver Copy of Contract

> **Art. 2682:** "The lease must be executed in writing..."

The landlord never provided the tenant with a copy of the signed contract. Since the contract was notarized, a certified copy (testimony) exists in the notary's protocol. The tenant can request it directly from the notary. However, the landlord's failure to deliver the copy aggravates the consent problem — the tenant cannot comply with terms that they neither understood at the time of signing nor have been able to review since.

**Key principle (Art. 21):** Ambiguity is resolved "in favor of the weaker party." The tenant — a foreigner without a copy of the contract, without proficiency in the Spanish language — is unquestionably the weaker party.

### 4.4 Tacit Renewal — Automatic Renewal of Lease

4. The landlord **accepted rent payments** at the original amount

The contract allegedly contains a clause making the tenant responsible for **all** repairs. This clause is **null and void** on multiple independent grounds:

#### Ground 1: Consent Never Existed (Art. 383(d))
The tenant did not know Spanish. Their will regarding this clause **does not exist**. The clause has no legal force.

#### Ground 2: Mandatory Law Cannot Be Waived (Art. 8, 2687)
Art. 2687 uses the phrase "is obligated, **even without express agreement**" — the landlord is obligated "even if there is no express agreement." This is mandatory law that cannot be circumvented by contract.

#### Ground 3: Violation of Public Order (Art. 13, 23)
Acts contrary to prohibitive laws or public order are null (Art. 13). Tenant protection is explicitly part of public order (Art. 23 — protection of the "economically, socially, or culturally weak").

#### Ground 4: Explicit Non-Waivability (Art. 2698, 2709)
Art. 2698 ends with "the provisions of this article are non-waivable." Art. 2709 states that the landlord's liability for habitability failures "cannot be waived in advance." These provisions demonstrate that the tenant-protective lease articles are non-waivable public order norms.

#### Ground 5: Exploitation of Ignorance (Art. 24)
Inserting an "all repairs" clause in a contract signed by a non-Spanish speaker who could not understand it constitutes exploitation of "extreme ignorance."

#### Ground 6: Partial Nullity (Art. 401)
Even if the contract is not entirely null, the "all repairs" clause can be severed and nullified independently while the rest of the contract subsists.

## 8. TENANT'S LEGAL ADVANTAGES AND LANDLORD'S WEAKNESSES

### ADVANTAGE 1: Art. 383(d) — The Language Bomb (Imprescriptible)
The tenant can invoke absolute nullity of any clause **at any time** — no statute of limitations. This means even years later, the tenant can challenge the "all repairs" clause. The landlord can never cure this defect without a new agreement properly translated with adequate consent.

### ADVANTAGE 2: Rent Prescription — The 2-Year Clock (Art. 2464)
If the landlord claims back rent or rent differences from more than 2 years ago, that claim is **prescribed**. Hard deadline, no exceptions.

### ADVANTAGE 3: Landlord Cannot Modify the Property During the Lease (Art. 2688)
The landlord cannot alter the property's form or interfere with legitimate use. If the landlord attempts to "renovate to evict" or begins construction to make it uninhabitable — that's a violation.

### ADVANTAGE 4: Strict Liability for Defects (Art. 2692)
The landlord is liable for **all defects** — even those unknown to them and those that arose during the lease. This is strict liability. The water heater that broke again, the toilet, the shower drain — the landlord is responsible regardless of knowledge.

### ADVANTAGE 5: Force Majeure Rent Reduction is NON-WAIVABLE (Art. 2698)
If circumstances partially prevent use of the property, the tenant can demand partial rent reduction. The contract CANNOT waive this. The phrase "the provisions of this article are non-waivable" is absolute.

### ADVANTAGE 6: Total Loss of Use = No Rent (Art. 2707)
If repairs render the property totally unusable, rent is suspended. If partial loss of use exceeds 2 months, the tenant can demand rescission.

### ADVANTAGE 7: Bond Trap (Art. 2740)
When tacit renewal activated, any third-party guarantees terminated automatically. The landlord lost their guarantee.

### ADVANTAGE 8: The Personal Use Exception Has Consequences (Art. 2738)
If the landlord claims "personal use" to evict and doesn't actually move in within 3 months, a judge can order the landlord to pay **up to 12 months' rent** as compensation.

### ADVANTAGE 9: Sale of Property Does Not Terminate Lease (Art. 2744)
If the property is sold, the lease subsists. A new owner inherits the tenant.

### ADVANTAGE 10: Building Owner is Liable for Building Damage (Art. 104)
The **building owner** is liable for damages arising from "lack of necessary repairs or construction defects." This is independent of the lease relationship — it is property law.

### ADVANTAGE 11: Management of Another's Affairs (Arts. 44, 48)
The tenant managed the landlord's property affairs by making repairs. This creates an independent legal basis for reimbursement, separate from the lease. Even if the lease is somehow interpreted in the landlord's favor, management of affairs provides a backup claim.

### ADVANTAGE 12: Art. 2694 Final Paragraph — Improvements Override Contract
Art. 2694 ends with: "The improvements referred to in sections II and III must be paid by the landlord, **notwithstanding any stipulation in the contract that the improvements shall remain for the benefit of the leased property.**" Even if the contract says improvements belong to the landlord, the landlord STILL has to pay when the lease ends by their fault or in an indefinite-term lease.

### ADVANTAGE 13: The 15-Day Clause is Null — You Have 2 Months
The contract says 15 days to vacate. The law says 2 months for urban property (Art. 2714). The law prevails. This clause is null under Arts. 8 and 13. The landlord cannot enforce a 15-day eviction timeline.

### ADVANTAGE 14: Challenge Property Ownership
If the landlord is not actually the property owner (she said it belongs to her parents), demand proof of authorization. A landlord who cannot prove authority to lease is in a very weak position.

### ADVANTAGE 15: Payment Consignment — Nuclear Defense Against Non-Payment Claims
If the landlord starts refusing rent to manufacture non-payment grounds (Art. 2741-I), the tenant can judicially deposit rent with the court. This proves willingness and ability to pay, completely neutralizing any non-payment claim.

## 10. STRATEGIC PRESSURE TOOLS

The following are legitimate legal tools available to tenants in Mexico. They are listed in order of what landlords typically fear most.

### 10.1 SAT Report (Tax Authority)
If the landlord is collecting rent without issuing **CFDI receipts** (digital tax invoices), she is violating Mexican tax law. A report to the SAT about undeclared rental income is completely legal, costs nothing, and creates enormous pressure. Landlords who collect informal payments without declaring income are extremely vulnerable.

### 10.2 Amparo Proceeding
An **amparo** is a constitutional remedy that can **completely halt eviction proceedings** while the case is reviewed. It is not expensive to file and can freeze the situation for months or years. A **preventive amparo** can be filed before eviction is even initiated if there is a credible reason to believe it is imminent. This completely eliminates the landlord's time advantage.

### 10.3 PROFECO Complaint
Filing a formal complaint with the **Federal Consumer Protection Agency** creates a public record of the dispute and triggers a conciliation process. Multiple complaints from different tenants (if other tenants exist in the building) multiply the pressure dramatically.

### 10.4 Notary Complaint (Notary College)
Filing a formal complaint with the **Quintana Roo Notary College** against the notary who processed the contract costs nothing and creates significant pressure. A notary under professional investigation becomes very cooperative. This also forces the landlord's documentation ally to become a potential legal liability.

### 10.5 Municipal Habitability Inspection (Complaint)
Filing a **complaint** with the Cancun municipal authorities citing habitability violations (broken water heater, non-functional toilet, drainage issues) triggers an official inspection. If the property fails, the landlord faces fines and an official repair order — completely independent of the civil dispute.

### 10.6 Notarial Challenge (Nuclear Option)
Formally challenging the notarial act itself before the Notary College AND the civil court simultaneously. Given that a non-Spanish speaker was rushed through a signing without an interpreter and never received a copy, this is a **legitimate challenge** that could make the entire contract voidable. The landlord then has no document to stand on.

### 10.7 Demand the Complete Notarial File
In litigation, the landlord must present the original instrument. Demanding the complete **notarial file** forces all irregularities in the notarial process into the open — and given your situation, there are likely many.

### 10.8 Utility Transfer Strategy
If utilities are in the landlord's name, she could theoretically use non-payment or service cuts as leverage. Transferring CFE (electricity) and CAPA (water) to your name removes this advantage. **However:** obtain your copy of the contract from the notary first to verify whether the lease restricts this. The **account holder change** process at CFE and CAPA only requires valid ID, proof of address, and the current account number.

### 10.9 What the Landlord Fears Most (In Order)
1. SAT investigation into undeclared rental income
2. Amparo filing that freezes timelines
3. PROFECO complaint creating a public record
4. Notary complaint threatening their license
5. Municipal habitability inspection
6. **A tenant who clearly knows all of the above**
