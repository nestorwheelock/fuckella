## 9. THE PROMISSORY NOTES (PAGARES) PROBLEM

### Why Promissory Notes Are a Dealbreaker

Clause 2 requires the tenant to sign **12 promissory notes** (pagarés) — one for each month of rent.

**What are pagarés?**

Promissory notes are **autonomous negotiable instruments** governed by the Mexican General Law of Negotiable Instruments and Credit Operations (Ley General de Títulos y Operaciones de Crédito). They are NOT governed by the Civil Code.

**Why they're dangerous:**

1. **Independent enforceability.** Each promissory note is a standalone debt obligation. Even if the lease is terminated, disputed, or declared null, the promissory notes remain enforceable as commercial instruments.

2. **Summary commercial proceedings.** Pagarés can be enforced through "juicio ejecutivo mercantil" — a fast-track commercial proceeding where the debtor's assets can be seized quickly. The tenant cannot raise most lease-related defenses in commercial proceedings.

3. **Separation from the lease.** If the landlord sues on the promissory notes, the tenant cannot easily argue "the property had defects" or "the rent was inflated" because those are Civil Code defenses in a Commercial Code proceeding.

4. **Transferability.** The landlord can **sell or endorse** the promissory notes to a third party (a debt collector, for example). That third party can then sue the tenant on the notes, and the tenant cannot raise defenses against the original landlord.

5. **12 months of exposure.** If the tenant pays rent by bank transfer (as required), the promissory notes should theoretically be returned for each month paid. But Clause 2 only provides for return of promissory notes in case of "early return of possession." There is no explicit provision for returning promissory notes as each month's rent is paid. This means the landlord could hold ALL 12 notes even after rent has been paid, and potentially enforce them.

6. **Double collection risk.** The tenant pays rent by bank transfer AND signs promissory notes. If the landlord claims the bank transfer wasn't received (or attributes it to something else), they can enforce the promissory note for the same month — collecting twice.

### The Prior Promissory Notes — STATUS: RESOLVED

Each prior year, the tenant signed 12 promissory notes as part of the annual contract. Each year, after all payments were made, the landlord returned the original pagarés to the tenant. This cycle repeated for each year of the tenancy.

**This year (Year 6), the tenant did not sign the new contract — and therefore did not sign new promissory notes.** There are no outstanding pagarés in the landlord's possession.

**This is critically important:** The promissory note vulnerability is CLOSED. The landlord has no commercial fast-track enforcement weapon. If she were to refuse to return pagarés in the future (or claim she still held prior ones), that would constitute **fraud** — presenting satisfied debt instruments for collection is a criminal offense under Mexican law.

**Compared to Tacit Renewal Status Quo:**
- Under the current arrangement, there are NO outstanding promissory notes.
- Rent is paid by bank transfer with a clear electronic record.
- There is no fast-track commercial enforcement mechanism.
- The landlord returned all prior pagarés after each year's payments were completed.

**Recommendation:** Promissory notes are an absolute dealbreaker in any new contract. Under no circumstances should the tenant sign new pagarés. The fact that prior pagarés were returned confirms the landlord acknowledged full payment — this is additional evidence of the tenant's consistent on-time payment history.

## 12. FEDERAL & STATE CODE RESEARCH: VERIFIED LEGAL PROVISIONS

### Background: Debunking the "2025 Rental Reform" Myth

A widely-circulated blog article (Mexico Custom Homes, Feb 2025) claimed that Mexico enacted nationwide rental law reforms in 2025 including inflation-linked rent caps, digital lease registries, and enhanced tenant protections. **This is false.** Research confirms:

- The reforms described are **CDMX (Mexico City) ONLY** — enacted by the local legislature, not the federal Congress
- The Federal Civil Code's last reform (DOF 14-11-2025, "Reform 60") is about **procedural harmonization** with the Código Nacional de Procedimientos Civiles y Familiares — NOT about rental protections
- The Quintana Roo Civil Code's last reform (POE 31-05-2023) reformed **disability/incapacity articles** — NO rental articles were changed in 2023
- **Federal Art. 13(III) confirms: rental contracts are governed by the law of the property's location** — i.e., Quintana Roo state law, not federal or CDMX law

**However**, the existing provisions in both the QR Civil Code and the Federal Civil Code are already very strong for this tenant. The research revealed several powerful provisions not previously cited in this analysis.

### The 5-Year Threshold: Art. 2708 (NEW — Not Previously Analyzed)

This is a significant new discovery. **Art. 2708 of the QR Civil Code** provides that when a lease has lasted more than 5 years AND the tenant has made improvements:

1. **Preference right for renewal:** The tenant has priority to renew the lease under equal conditions
2. **Right of first refusal on sale (Art. 1999):** If the landlord wants to sell the property, the tenant must be offered the opportunity to purchase it first, under the same terms as any third-party buyer

**Application to this case:**
- Tenancy began March 2021. As of March 2026 = **exactly 5 years**
- The tenant has made documented improvements: roof repair, plumbing repairs, water pump, faucets, floor repair, bed frame repair
- These improvements were substantial (the roof repair alone was a multi-day project)
- **This means the tenant is approaching or has reached the 5-year threshold that triggers these additional protections**

If the landlord tries to terminate the lease to sell the property, the tenant has a legal right of first refusal. If the landlord tries to replace the tenant with someone else, the tenant has priority for renewal. These rights exist by operation of law — they don't depend on the contract.
